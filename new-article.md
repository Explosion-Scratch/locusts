Locusts - When we hear this word we think of a {biblical plague of swarming creatures}[hoverimg-https://www.geowyo.com/uploads/8/4/7/8/84786270/editor/gg2.png?1595113332], taking to the skies, destroying crops, and spreading disease, but for those in most parts of the world, they are only that - a thought, a remnant of the past, however, for pioneers and Americans in the 1800s they were a devastating reality. Locusts ate crops at a massive scale, they {stopped trains}[hoverimg-http://www.lyndonirwin.com/hopppic2.jpg] and caused thousands to die of starvation. Once, trillions of insects were eating at everything green on the Great Plains, then a few decades later they were extinct. What are locusts and what happened to them? Could they return again?

> "They began, toward night, dropping to the earth and it seemed as if we were in a big snowstorm where the air was filled with enormous-sized flakes. They devoured every green thing but the prairie grass". Mary Lyon, a pioneer in Kansas recalling the Swam of 1874[https://lisawallerrogers.com/2024/05/05/the-bug-that-ate-topeka-2/].

In the swarm Lyon recounts, an estimated 12.5 trillion Rocky Mountain locusts descended upon the Great Plains. Amateur meteorologist and physician Albert Childers measured this swarm as 198,000 square miles- [larger than the state of California](RILEY'S MAP). Albert's Swarm[alberts swarm], named after Albert Childers contained 12.5 trillion individual insects roughly 2,000 insects for every human being alive on Earth today. The _Guinness Book of World Records_ estimates that this was the greatest concentration of animals in history[worldrecord]. Some reports placed the swarm at a mile or more in height. It blocked out the sun for days, and halted trains. The crushed insects' bodies made the rails so slippery that the locomotives lost traction[https://digital.outdoornebraska.gov/nebraskaland-magazine/clouds-of-grasshoppers-in-1874]. This swarm, known as Albert's Swarm, after Albert Child who described it, was the last of its size.

The species that plagued these settlers - the {Rocky Mountain Locust}[hoverimg-https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a8/Melanoplus_spretusAnnReportAgExpStaUM1902B.jpg] - (_Melanoplus spretus_) is now gone, the last specimen collected in 1904 only 40 years after the estimate of 12.5 trillion. They were so numerous that very few specimens were ever collected, nor were they extensively studied, driving modern {insect-historians}[hoverimg-https://m.media-amazon.com/images/I/81QOkf8RSIL._AC_UF1000,1000_QL80_.jpg] to collect samples from glaciers that had engulfed them centuries ago[https://www.hcn.org/issues/issue-243/the-death-of-the-super-hopper/]. {Not only did they disappear rapidly, the locusts also appeared without warning, only initially described in [1800](in Manitoba Canada ["Locusts were reported in Manitoba, Canada, during 1800 and 1808" - Rocky Mountain Locust in Texas])}[hovercard - The locusts were also relatively unknown to the Native Americans. ## Charles Valentine Riley and the start of federally funded science

Amidst the disastrous devastation, the country was in need of a solution, however, there were no experts on Locusts at the time["Scientific expertise and governmental assistance were nonexistant" - The Rocky Mountain locust in Texas], [no known ways to prevent them before they arrived](It was unknown at the time where the Locusts' breeding ground were - As they swarmed they laid eggs in the ground, though few of these ended up successfully hatching) and no way to stop them once they engrossed the land. As a result, farmers resorted to invention over despair, though such efforts were ultimately inadequate. Farmers attempted:

- Digging trenches ahead of the marching [nymphs](Juvenile locusts that have just hatched) and filling them with fire or water
- Dragging tar soaked ropes across the ground to trap [crawling nymphs](Nymphs cannot fly until they mature, though they march parallel to each other for miles (described in detail later, feel free to skip!))
- {Creating a wheeled "hopper-dozer"}[hovercard - The Hopper-Dozer was one of the most popular machines used to combat the locusts, essentially a narrow sheet-metal scoop that fed the locusts into kerosene or tar [Cite https://todayinhistory.blog/2018/10/20/october-20-1937-alberts-swarm/]. This strategy was very effective at killing nymphs before they turned into adults.]
- Spreading loose hay in their path then igniting it once the nymphs had crawled on
- Underground explosions beneath identified egg beds
- Poisoned bait of arsenic laced bran or sawdust (scattered over fields).

None of these methods worked effectively against the trillions of locusts. The hay burning consumed limited crops farmers could not spare, and the arsenic was dangerous to handle and indiscriminate in its killing. Egg beds could not be identified reliably enough for the underground explosions, and the grasshopper catcher simply couldn't kill them all.

Eventually, however, governmental funding towards scientific endeavors began to have an impact. {At the center of this response stood Charles Valentine Riley}[hovercard- Fun fact about Riley - He saved the French Wine Industry in 1871. ## Locust Biology

It is at this point that one may be wondering about what a locust even is. To understand the danger that locusts caused, where they came from, where they went, and why they are so mean to our crops must first look to their biology.

### Life Cycle

Locusts and grasshoppers are one and the same. The Rocky Mountain Locust that plagued the Midwest, when not in swarms was merely a shy, avoidant green grasshopper. A locust is not a separate species, but rather a grasshopper that has undergone a physiological transformation triggered by its environment. Select species of the short horned grasshoppers family have this ability. <HoverImg https://hopperwiki.org/images/c/c4/Australian_plague_locust_life_cycle_diagram.jpg>Locusts lay their eggs in the ground, in (as Riley discovered) dry, sandy, fertile environments. After around two weeks of producing offspring the adults begin to die off[Flint ML (1998) Pests of the Garden and Small Farm. 2nd ed. Oakland: Univ. Calif. Agric. Nat. Res. Publ. 3332.]. The embryo then grows rapidly and hatch within two months.</HoverImg> The babies known as nymphs resemble small adults (a process known as [incomplete metamorphosis](Which has three major stages: egg, nymph and adult)) undergo five or six sheddings known as 'instars' upon each of which they progress towards becoming a fully grown grasshopper. As nymphs, they are wingless until adulthood. During the final moult, the new adult must spend {several hours waiting for the exoskeleton to harden and for the wings to complete development}[hoverimg- https://images.fineartamerica.com/images/artworkimages/mediumlarge/2/1-moulting-locust-from-swarm-in-palestine-in-1915-library-of-congress-american-colony-in-jerusalem-collectionscience-photo-library.jpg]. The behavior and triggers of their external environment decides whether the organism is a grasshopper or a locust. The majority of damage to crops done by locusts is done by late stage nymphs and adults.

### Polyphenism - The plight of an angry grasshopper

This transformation is known as [**polyphenism**](From Greek _poly_ (many) + _phaino_ (to show)) - a phenomenon where a single [genotype](An organism's genetic material) produces distinct [phenotypes](The observable traits of an organism - its outward appearance and behavior) in response to environmental stimuli.

There are two resulting states of behavior for grasshoppers, separating them into 'grasshopper' or 'locust' categories. The solitary phase characteristic of grasshoppers, and the gregarious phase of locusts.

#### The Solitary Phase

During the solitary phase, grasshoppers in low density environments are crowdphobic, sluggish and green or brown, blending into grass. Grasshoppers in this stage are slow-moving and actively avoid one another. Additionally, they are far more cautious, refusing to eat unfamiliar plants, and have an [olfactory](Smell) system configured to detect predators[100yrsphase]. For a solitary grasshopper, there is no group to dilute the predation risk. Most species of grasshoppers solely live in this solitary phase, lacking the ability to become locusts. Around twenty species of {short-horned grasshoppers}[hovercard-The alternate 'long-horned' grasshoppers are more commonly known as katydids or bush crickets. These species, though they can possess swarm behavior akin to the gregarious phase of the locust, do not have the ability to transition from one to the other. ] however, possess the capability for [density dependent phase polyphenism](The type seen in locusts - The ability to switch between a solitary and gregarious form based on population density.)

#### The Transformation into a Locust

Then comes the transformation to a locust. This transformation begins with crowding. Drought followed by abundant rain produces a flush of new vegetation, enabling rapid breeding. As the population grows, grasshoppers are concentrated into shrinking patches of suitable habitat as they begin to consume the grass. They then are presented with many different stimuli that trigger their transformation[Simpson study - https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19179529/]:

1. Touch - Mechanosensory stimulation of the [hind femora (femurs)](![Hind femora](https://www.amentsoc.org/images/insect-leg-structure.jpg)). Being repeatedly bumped on the back legs by other grasshoppers triggers a neurochemical cascade in the [thoracic ganglia](The central nervous system nodes that control locomotion, not the brain).
2. Smell - Airborne pheromones from nearby locusts on antennal chemoreceptors
3. Sight - The visual stimulus of other locusts moving in the peripheral visual field

Any one of these stimuli applied consistently is enough to initiate the phase change (for instance, stroking the leg of a nymph [Simpson and burrows study]). All of these triggers cause a release of serotonin. This serotonin release triggers the physical transformation, and injecting serotonin or blocking it [induces](A single solitary locust in the solitary grasshopper stage injected with serotonin over the course of a few hours begins acting gregariously and then during its next moult shifts color and appearance, becoming a locust.) or [prevents the transform respectively[Simpson and Barrows]](Even when under very crowded conditions that would regularly cause the transformation to the gregarious stage.).

#### The Gregarious Phase

The next phase, the gregarious phase, is the social phase in which locusts swarm, destroy crops, and fly long distances. Gregarious desert locusts have brains that are 30% larger than their solitarious counterparts despite their overall body size. In addition to their behavior shifts from solitary and cautious to swarming and social, many other physical aspects change in the gregarious locust. Their color shifts from camouflage green or brown to a [conspicuous yellow and black](This coloration is an anti-predator warning known as aposmatism. Gregarious locusts rely on group dilution to reduce individual predation risk.). Their wings lengthen, flight muscles strengthen, and body proportions shift towards greater investment in locomotion. Their brains also grow and their optic lobes expand for tracking the movements of swarm mates in dense formations.

The behavior of the locust also shifts in the gregarious phase. Whereas solitary grasshoppers are nocturnal, locusts are diurnal. Additionally, their walking speed increases, their body posture changes (from crouched to held high off the ground), and grooming behavior diminishes. Additionally, the caution that characterizes solitarious feeding grasshoppers vanishes, and gregarious locusts consume toxic plants they would have previously avoided[100yrsphase].

The mechanism underlying this shift is known as DNA methylation, where a gene is suppressed by adding a [methyl group](–CH3 - A hydrophobic and very stable addition that adds bulk to a molecule) to it. This addition prevents the cellular machinery from reading the gene and therefore that gene from being expressed in the organism. This methylation is [epigenetic](A heritable change.) - That is, the gregarious state is heritable. In solitarious mothers, [the gene](The Dop1 dopamine receptor[100yrsphase]) is heavily methylated and thus suppressed. In gregarious mothers this methylation is reduced (due to the shift to a locust) and eggs carry this trait forward. Nymphs then exhibit gregarious social behavior even before being exposed to crowding[100yrsphase].

### Canibalistic Marching

One of the predominant questions facing researchers of locusts is why they march. Locusts [march in parallel lines as flightless youth]([This paper](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.1125142) is an interesting study explaining how locusts begin to spontaneously form parallel lines) across vast stretches of landscape, coordinating into a moving, hungry, swarm that covers the ground like water. Behavioral ecologist Stephen Simpson has proposed the _cannibalistic forced march hypothesis_ [Simpson], that is, the forward motion of a locust formed is essentially sustained by each individual's imperative to avoid being eaten by the locust behind it.

When locust nymphs are deprived of [protein and salt (nutrients that begin to be depleted as the locusts swarm)](This is also the reason why locusts go after the salt from sweat on the handles of farm equipment), they begin to cannibalize the slower and weaker individuals. Due to this combination of factors, the locusts[Bazazi]:

- Align their body axis with neighbors (parallel) to minimize the chances of a side on attack and present their narrowest possible profile to the individual behind.
- March forward to bite and feed on the abdomen of the locust immediately ahead.
- Accelerate when nutrient deprived, creating a self-reinforcing march.

This combined behavior means that each individual's optimal strategy of moving forward, staying aligned, and not stopping, happens to produce an {aggregate of coordinated marching behavior}[hovercard- Flock behavior usually follows 3 rules, that can be simulated in the [Boid's Algorithm](https://eater.net/boids). Coherence (each organism moves towards another - e.g. Locusts' marching), separation (they attempt to not run into one another), and alignment (aligning to the organism in front or behind). All of these properties are satisfied in the case of locusts, as well as for many birds, fish, insects, and mammals]. For each locust it is a high stakes arms (or [legs](Grasshopper's legs are called _saltatorial_ legs - From Latin _saltare_ (to leap / dance))) race.

## Elegy to a plague - how the Rocky Mountain Locust finally fell

The locust swarms eventually diminished and by the 1890s they were rare. The last confirmed specimen was collected in 1902[https://www.hcn.org/issues/issue-243/the-death-of-the-super-hopper]. Nobody at the time registered this as an extinction event as the locust was so abundant, familiar, and terrifying that few specimens had been systematically studied["Curiously, for a creature that existed in the millions of tons, very few of them were collected at the time" - https://daily.jstor.org/the-long-lost-locust/]. The most common interpretation was luck or an answered prayer. Over a century later, entomologist Jeffrey Lockwood proposed a hypothesis for their disappearance[Lockwood].

The Rocky Mountain locust did not breed everywhere, but rather {selected very specific habitats for egg deposition: fertile, well drained soils of river valleys}[background image of map from beginning]. The locusts had chosen the best land in the Rockies for farming and over the next few decades, the conversion of river bottom habitat from native grassland to irrigated cropland destroyed the locusts breeding grounds.

As Riley discovered, irrigation caused eggs to rot and die, and plowing the land destroyed likely trillions of underground egg beds. Over the next several decades Riley highly advocated for the regular flooding of farmland home to locusts["This [irrigation] is feasible in much of the country subject to locust ravages, especially in the mountain regions, where, except in exceptionally favorable locations, agriculture can be successfully carried on only by its aid" - Lockwood quoting Riley]. The settlers of these areas did not intend to exterminate the Rocky Mountain locust, but rather to grow alfalfa and hay in the only arable land available to them. Though the locusts had a huge migratory range stretching all the way to the eastern seaboard, its reproductive range was only a handful of river valleys in Wyoming and Montana. Once plowed, irrigated and trampled by livestock the species had nowhere left to lay eggs. The planting of alfalfa and hay may have also had an impact, as these were the only crops left after a locust plague as the insects had a distaste for them["Of leguminous plants the pods are preferred to the leaves, which are often passed by. . . . The dislike these insects show for leguminous plants is well known, and a crop of peas will often succeed where they abound, when all else is ruined" Lockwood quoting Riley].

The Rocky Mountain locust's extinction also had an interesting consequency beyond entomology. The Eskimo Curlew (_Numenius borealis_) was a shorebird that migrated annually between its Arctic breeding grounds and the grasslands of South America. This route passed through the Great Plains where it fed heavily on Rocky Mountain locust eggs and nymphs[fishandwildlife]. The curlew was originally in populations so dense that its flocks were mistaken for passenger pigeons. They were also easy to hunt as survivors circled back to the wounded. When the Rocky Mountain locust disappeared, the Eskimo lost its primary food source along the migration corridor. One of the first species listed under the Endangered Species Preservation at in 1966, no confirmed sighting has occurred since 1963.

### Bison Theory

An alternate theory proposed by Canadian entomologist Paul Riegert suggested that perhaps the extinction of the bison may have been causally related[Lockwood]. He argues that localized overgrazing by bison herds created short and [disturbed grass conditions ideal the nymphs](Lockwood found that "The females avoided very loose soil and moist ground" which is the opposite of what Riegert assumed), and that bison dung served as a food source for the newly hatched. This hypothesis fell apart under further scrutiny however, as Lockwood demonstrated that the Rocky Mountain locust actually preferred well-drained and undisturbed soil over loose trampled ground[Lockwood]. Additionally, bison were quickly replaced by cattle, which should have provided an equivalent habitat.

A similar earlier hypothesis proposed by Irving Cantrall and Frank Young in 1954 that the extermination of the bison may have _allowed_ locust populations to flourish by removing a competitive animal, (though historical records also disproved this)[Lockwood].

The carrying capacity of rangeland for modern-day grasshoppers is around 10 individuals per square yard. Using this figure—which is probably quite conservative, as we’re considering the locust during an outbreak—we’d end up with 15 trillion insects, or a couple thousand locusts for every person currently on the earth." - Lockwood
