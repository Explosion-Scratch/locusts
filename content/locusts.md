Locusts—when we hear this word we think of a <BackgroundImage asset="biblical-plague-locusts">biblical plague of swarming creatures</BackgroundImage>, taking to the skies, destroying crops, and spreading disease. But for those in most parts of the world, they are only that—a thought, a remnant of the past. However, for pioneers and Americans in the 1800s they were a devastating reality. Locusts ate crops on a massive scale; they <BackgroundImage asset="stopped-trains-locusts">stopped trains</BackgroundImage> and caused thousands to die of starvation. Once, trillions of insects were eating at everything green on the Great Plains, then a few decades later they were extinct. What are locusts and what happened to them? Could they return again?

> "They began, toward night, dropping to the earth and it seemed as if we were in a big snowstorm where the air was filled with enormous-sized flakes. They devoured every green thing but the prairie grass". Mary Lyon, a pioneer in Kansas, recalling the Swarm of 1874<Citation source="lisa_rogers" quote-index="0" />.

Racing through the skies and landing upon crops, people and animals, were Rocky Mountain locusts. Descending upon the Great Plains, the swarm measured as 198,000 square miles—[larger than the state of California](The map shown below was the first to map out locusts' range.)<Citation source="rileys_map" />. This swarm numbered **12.5 trillion individual insects**—roughly 2,000 insects for every human being alive on Earth today. The _Guinness Book of World Records_ estimates that this was the greatest concentration of animals in history<Citation source="animal_diversity" />. Some reports placed the swarm at a mile or more in height. It blocked out the sun for days, [halted trains](The crushed insects' bodies made the rails so slippery that the locomotives lost traction<Citation source="nebraska_outdoor" />) and caused widespread famine. This swarm, known as Albert's Swarm, was the latest and largest of many though it would also be one of the last.

![Charles Valentine Riley's 1877 map of the locust plague, showing the permanent, sub-permanent, and occasional breeding regions mapped by the United States Entomological Commission.](/assets/images/rileys-locust-map.jpg)

The species that plagued these settlers—the <BackgroundImage asset="rocky-mountain-locust-specimen">Rocky Mountain Locust</BackgroundImage>—(_Melanoplus spretus_) is now gone. The last specimen was collected in 1902, only 40 years after the estimate of 12.5 trillion. Because they were so numerous, very few specimens were ever collected or extensively studied, driving modern <BackgroundImage asset="locust-book-cover">insect-historians</BackgroundImage> to collect samples from glaciers that had engulfed them centuries ago<Citation source="hcn_super_hopper" />. <Hover content="The locusts were also relatively unknown to the Native Americans. Though small swarms had been encountered before, nothing of this size had ever come about. Such swarms were only spoken of as a mythological threat in distant oral traditions. Some tribes believed that the insects were a curse brought by the white invaders.">Not only did they vanish without a trace, but they also appeared without warning, only initially described in [1800](in Manitoba Canada &quot;Locusts were reported in Manitoba, Canada, during 1800 and 1808&quot;—Rocky Mountain Locust in Texas)</Hover><Citation source="casto" quote-index="0" />. To tell the story of the Rocky Mountain Locust is to tell of one of the most numerous animals in history, the desperate pioneers who tried everything to stave off a biblical plague, and of a remarkable unification of research, legislation, and community.

_What follows is my best attempt to tell the story of the rise and fall of the most successful American pioneer—the Rocky Mountain Locust._

## March madness, fall famine

### Impacts on Settlers

These huge swarms of locusts could consume over 4000 pounds of vegetation per hour<Citation source="nebraska_history" />, marching and swarming across vast swaths of land. To the pioneers they were devastating; they consumed flesh<Citation source="lockwood" quote-index="5" />, clothes<Citation source="lisa_rogers" quote-index="0" />, leather, and even gorged themselves on the salt left on farm implements from human sweat<Citation source="nebraska_history" quote-index="0" />. Not even chickens or their eggs were safe to eat, as their meat ended up covered in a [red, pungent oil](this substance was caloptine — a transparent, reddish-brown oil described by Riley as having a "very pungent and penetrating odor." It accumulated in the tissues of birds that gorged on locusts, rendering their flesh unpalatable to humans) after gorging themselves on locusts. Water supplies were polluted as millions of drowned insects decomposed in creeks and wells. Cows and chickens that survived produced tainted meat and eggs for weeks afterward<Citation source="nebraska_outdoor" />. Albert's Swarm caused an estimated 50 million dollars of destruction—<Money from="50000000" year="1874" mode="result">$1.6 billion</Money> in today's dollars<Citation source="nebraska_history" />. This accounted for the destruction of 74% of crops (by value)<Citation source="nebraska_history" />, leaving tens of thousands in immediate danger of starvation<Citation source="nebraska_outdoor" />.

## Charles Valentine Riley and the start of federally funded science

Amidst the disastrous devastation, the country was in need of a solution; however, there were no experts on Locusts at the time<Citation source="casto" quote-index="0" />, [no known ways to prevent them before they arrived](It was unknown at the time where the Locusts' breeding grounds were—as they swarmed they laid eggs in the ground, though few of these ended up successfully hatching) and no way to stop them once they engulfed the land. As a result, farmers resorted to invention over despair, though such efforts were ultimately inadequate. Farmers attempted:

- Digging trenches ahead of the marching [nymphs](Juvenile locusts that have just hatched) and filling them with fire or water
- Dragging tar-soaked ropes across the ground to trap [crawling nymphs](Nymphs cannot fly until they mature, though they march parallel to each other for miles (described in detail later, feel free to skip!))
- <Hover content="The Hopper-Dozer was one of the most popular machines used to combat the locusts, essentially a narrow sheet-metal scoop that fed the locusts into kerosene or tar. This strategy was very effective at killing nymphs before they turned into adults.">Creating a wheeled "hopper-dozer"</Hover><Citation source="today_history" />
- Spreading loose hay in their path then igniting it once the nymphs had crawled on
- Underground explosions beneath identified egg beds
- Poisoned bait of arsenic-laced bran or sawdust (scattered over fields).

None of these methods worked effectively against the trillions of locusts. The hay burning consumed limited crops farmers could not spare, and the arsenic was dangerous to handle and indiscriminate in its killing. Egg beds could not be identified reliably enough for the underground explosions, and the grasshopper catcher simply couldn't kill them all.

Eventually, however, governmental funding toward scientific endeavors began to have an impact. <Hover content="Fun fact about Riley—He saved the French Wine Industry in 1871. French vineyards were collapsing due to an aphid-like insect that caused root tumors. He successfully helped the French graft their vines onto American roots and saved 6 million acres of vineyards. He was then awarded a gold medal by the French government for this effort in 1873.">At the center of this response stood Charles Valentine Riley</Hover>. Born in London's Chelsea district, he served as the state entomologist of Missouri before being appointed to lead the newly formed United States Entomological Commission in 1876<Citation source="lockwood" />. Riley was the first to map the locusts' range and migration, discovering a "Permanent Zone" in the Rocky Mountains. He debunked popular myths of the time, proving that swarms were not led by 'kings or queens', and that prairie fires did not cause the droughts that summoned the locusts. He also discovered that though locusts seemed to lay their eggs anywhere, only eggs laid in dry, sandy soils were viable enough to hatch. In a further unorthodox attempt to combat starvation on the frontier, Riley heavily advocated eating the locusts<Citation source="lockwood" quote="Nearly five pages of the commission’s first report were devoted to an argument for consuming locusts" />, working with a caterer to prepare locusts, <BackgroundImage asset="chicken-fricassee">fricassee</BackgroundImage> and soup, [claiming that they were as nutritious as oysters](He even took fried specimens to Europe to prove their palatability.)<Citation source="lockwood" quote="Riley took fried specimens to France and England, where “they were tasted and reported better than expected.”" />.

Riley meticulously documented the species' life cycle, egg-laying preferences, natural predators, and the permanent region in the Rocky Mountains. Riley's most groundbreaking discovery for the control of the locust, though, was that ["when irrigation is practicable, as it is in some of the ravaged parts of Colorado, let the ground be thoroughly inundated for a few days, and the eggs will lose vitality and rot."](Here Riley was only describing pest mitigation, but he unknowingly helped spur the mechanism that would force the locust into extinction—the saturation and conversion of farmlands of the breeding grounds of the locust to cropland.)<Citation source="lockwood" quote-index="1" />

## Locust Biology

It is at this point that one may be wondering about what a locust even is. To understand the danger that locusts caused, where they came from, where they went, and why they were so devastating to our crops, we must first look to their biology.

### Life Cycle

Locusts and grasshoppers are one and the same. The Rocky Mountain Locust that plagued the Midwest, when not in swarms, was merely a shy, avoidant green grasshopper. A locust is not a separate species, but rather a grasshopper that has undergone a physiological transformation triggered by its environment. Select species of the short-horned grasshopper family have this ability. <BackgroundImage asset="australian-plague-locust-life-cycle">Locusts lay their eggs in the ground, in (as Riley discovered) dry, sandy, fertile environments. After around two weeks of producing offspring the adults begin to die off<Citation source="flint" />. The embryos then grow rapidly and hatch within two months.</BackgroundImage><Citation source="hopperwiki_lifecycle_diagram" /> The nymphs, which resemble small adults (a process known as [incomplete metamorphosis](Which has three major stages: egg, nymph and adult)), undergo five or six sheddings known as 'instars' upon each of which they progress toward becoming a fully grown grasshopper. As nymphs, they are wingless until adulthood. During the final molt, the new adult must spend <BackgroundImage asset="moulting-locust-palestine-1915">several hours waiting for the exoskeleton to harden and for the wings to complete development</BackgroundImage>. The behavior and triggers of their external environment decide whether the organism is a grasshopper or a locust. The majority of damage to crops done by locusts is done by late-stage nymphs and adults.

### Polyphenism—the plight of an angry grasshopper

This transformation is known as [**polyphenism**](From Greek _poly_ (many) + _phaino_ (to show))—a phenomenon where a single [genotype](An organism's genetic material) produces distinct [phenotypes](The observable traits of an organism—its outward appearance and behavior) in response to environmental stimuli.

There are two resulting states of behavior for grasshoppers, which separate them into 'grasshopper' or 'locust' categories: the solitary phase characteristic of grasshoppers, and the gregarious phase of locusts.

#### The Solitary Phase

During the solitary phase, grasshoppers in low-density environments are averse to crowding, sluggish and green or brown, blending into grass. Grasshoppers in this stage are slow-moving and actively avoid one another. Additionally, they are far more cautious, refusing to eat unfamiliar plants, and have an [olfactory](Smell) system configured to detect predators<Citation source="pfluger_braunig" />. For a solitary grasshopper, there is no group to dilute the predation risk. Most species of grasshoppers solely live in this solitary phase, lacking the ability to become locusts. Around twenty species of <Hover content="The alternate 'long-horned' grasshoppers are more commonly known as katydids or bush crickets. These species, though they can possess swarm behavior akin to the gregarious phase of the locust, do not have the ability to transition from one to the other.">short-horned grasshoppers</Hover>, however, possess the capability for [density-dependent phase polyphenism](The type seen in locusts—the ability to switch between a solitary and gregarious form based on population density.)

#### The Transformation into a Locust

Then comes the transformation to a locust. This transformation begins with crowding. Drought followed by abundant rain produces a flush of new vegetation, enabling rapid breeding. As the population grows, grasshoppers are concentrated into shrinking patches of suitable habitat as they begin to consume the grass. They then are presented with many different stimuli that trigger their transformation<Citation source="anstey_serotonin" />:

1. Touch—mechanosensory stimulation of the [hind femora (femurs)](The large jumping-leg segment most often bumped during crowding). Being repeatedly bumped on the back legs by other grasshoppers triggers a neurochemical cascade in the [thoracic ganglia](The central nervous system nodes that control locomotion, not the brain).
2. Smell—airborne pheromones from nearby locusts on antennal chemoreceptors
3. Sight—the visual stimulus of other locusts moving in the peripheral visual field

Any one of these stimuli applied consistently is enough to initiate the phase change (for instance, stroking the leg of a nymph<Citation source="anstey_serotonin" />). All of these triggers cause a release of serotonin. This serotonin release triggers the physical transformation, and injecting serotonin or blocking it [induces](A single solitary locust in the solitary grasshopper stage injected with serotonin over the course of a few hours begins acting gregariously and then during its next molt shifts color and appearance, becoming a locust.) or [prevents the transformation, respectively](Even when under very crowded conditions that would regularly cause the transformation to the gregarious stage.)<Citation source="anstey_serotonin" />.

The transformation into a gregarious locust is a remarkable one. Their brains grow 30% larger than their solitarious counterparts despite their overall body size. In addition to their behavior shifting from solitary and cautious to swarming and social, many other physical aspects change in the gregarious locust. Their color shifts from camouflage green or brown to a [conspicuous yellow and black](This coloration is an anti-predator warning known as aposmatism. Gregarious locusts rely on group dilution to reduce individual predation risk.). Their wings lengthen, flight muscles strengthen, and body proportions shift toward greater investment in locomotion. Their brains also grow and their optic lobes expand for tracking the movements of swarm mates in dense formations.

The behavior of the locust also shifts in the gregarious phase. Whereas solitary grasshoppers are nocturnal, locusts are diurnal. Additionally, their walking speed increases, their body posture changes (from crouched to held high off the ground), and grooming behavior diminishes. Additionally, the caution that characterizes solitarious feeding grasshoppers vanishes, and gregarious locusts consume toxic plants they would have previously avoided<Citation source="pfluger_braunig" />.

The mechanism underlying this shift is known as DNA methylation, where a gene is suppressed by adding a [methyl group](–CH3—A hydrophobic and very stable addition that adds bulk to a molecule) to it. This addition prevents the cellular machinery from reading the gene and therefore that gene from being expressed in the organism. This methylation is [epigenetic](A heritable change.)—that is, the gregarious state is heritable. In solitarious mothers, [the gene](The Dop1 dopamine receptor) is heavily methylated and thus suppressed. In gregarious mothers this methylation is reduced (due to the shift to a locust) and eggs carry this trait forward. Nymphs then exhibit gregarious social behavior even before being exposed to crowding<Citation source="pfluger_braunig" />.

### Cannibalistic Marching

One of the predominant questions facing researchers of locusts is why they march. Locusts [march in parallel lines as flightless youth](This paper is an interesting study explaining how locusts begin to spontaneously form parallel lines) across vast stretches of landscape<Citation source="science_marching" />, coordinating into a moving, hungry, swarm that covers the ground like water. Behavioral ecologist Stephen Simpson has proposed the _cannibalistic forced march hypothesis_<Citation source="simpson_sword" />, that is, the forward motion of a locust swarm is essentially sustained by each individual's imperative to avoid being eaten by the locust behind it.

When locust nymphs are deprived of [protein and salt (nutrients that begin to be depleted as the locusts swarm)](This is also the reason why locusts go after the salt from sweat on the handles of farm equipment), they begin to cannibalize the slower and weaker individuals. Due to this combination of factors, the locusts<Citation source="bazazi_cannibalism" />:

- Align their body axis with neighbors (parallel) to minimize the chances of a side-on attack and present their narrowest possible profile to the individual behind.
- March forward to bite and feed on the abdomen of the locust immediately ahead.
- Accelerate when nutrient deprived, creating a self-reinforcing march.

This combined behavior means that each individual's optimal strategy of moving forward, staying aligned, and not stopping, happens to produce an <Hover content="Flock behavior usually follows 3 rules, that can be simulated in the [Boid's Algorithm](https://eater.net/boids). Coherence (each organism moves toward another—e.g. Locusts' marching), separation (they attempt to not run into one another), and alignment (aligning to the organism in front or behind). All of these properties are satisfied in the case of locusts, as well as for many birds, fish, insects, and mammals.">aggregate of coordinated marching behavior</Hover>. For each locust it is a high-stakes arms race (or [leg race](Grasshopper's legs are called _saltatorial_ legs—from Latin _saltare_ (to leap / dance))).

## Elegy to a plague—how the Rocky Mountain Locust finally fell

The locust swarms eventually diminished and by the 1890s they were rare. The last confirmed specimen was collected in 1902<Citation source="hcn_super_hopper" />. Nobody at the time registered this as an extinction event as the locust was so abundant, familiar, and terrifying that few specimens had been systematically studied<Citation source="jstor_daily" quote="Curiously, for a creature that existed in the millions of tons, very few of them were collected at the time" />. The most common interpretation was luck or an answered prayer. Over a century later, entomologist Jeffrey Lockwood proposed a hypothesis for their disappearance<Citation source="lockwood" />.

The Rocky Mountain locust did not breed everywhere, but rather <BackgroundImage asset="rileys-locust-map">selected very specific habitats for egg deposition: fertile, well-drained soils of river valleys</BackgroundImage><Citation source="lockwood" quote-index="0" />. The locusts had chosen the best land in the Rockies for farming and over the next few decades, the conversion of river bottom habitat from native grassland to irrigated cropland destroyed the locusts' breeding grounds.

As Riley discovered, irrigation caused eggs to rot and die, and plowing the land destroyed likely trillions of underground egg beds. Over the next several decades Riley highly advocated for the regular flooding of farmland home to locusts<Citation source="lockwood" quote="This [irrigation] is feasible in much of the country subject to locust ravages, especially in the mountain regions, where, except in exceptionally favorable locations, agriculture can be successfully carried on only by its aid" />. The settlers of these areas did not intend to exterminate the Rocky Mountain locust, but rather to grow alfalfa and hay in the only arable land available to them. Though the locusts had a huge migratory range stretching all the way to the eastern seaboard, its reproductive range was only a handful of river valleys in <Place lat="43.0759" lng="-107.2903" zoom="5" map-title="Wyoming and Montana">Wyoming and Montana</Place>. Once plowed, irrigated and trampled by livestock the species had nowhere left to lay eggs. The planting of alfalfa and hay may have also had an impact, as these were the only crops left after a locust plague as the insects had a distaste for them<Citation source="lockwood" quote="Of leguminous plants the pods are preferred to the leaves, which are often passed by. . . . The dislike these insects show for leguminous plants is well known, and a crop of peas will often succeed where they abound, when all else is ruined" />.

The Rocky Mountain locust's extinction also had an interesting consequence beyond entomology. The Eskimo Curlew (_Numenius borealis_) was a shorebird that migrated annually between its Arctic breeding grounds and the grasslands of South America. This route passed through the Great Plains where it fed heavily on Rocky Mountain locust eggs and nymphs<Citation source="eskimo_curlew" />. The curlew was originally in populations so dense that its flocks were mistaken for passenger pigeons. They were also easy to hunt as survivors circled back to the wounded. When the Rocky Mountain locust disappeared, the Eskimo Curlew lost its primary food source along the migration corridor. One of the first species listed under the Endangered Species Preservation Act in 1966, no confirmed sighting has occurred since 1963.

### Bison Theory

An alternate theory proposed by Canadian entomologist Paul Riegert suggested that perhaps the extinction of the bison may have been causally related<Citation source="lockwood" quote-index="2" />. He argues that localized overgrazing by bison herds created short and [disturbed grass conditions ideal for the nymphs](Lockwood found that "The females avoided very loose soil and moist ground" which is the opposite of what Riegert assumed), and that bison dung served as a food source for the newly hatched. This hypothesis fell apart under further scrutiny however, as Lockwood demonstrated that the Rocky Mountain locust actually preferred well-drained and undisturbed soil over loose trampled ground<Citation source="lockwood" quote-index="3" />. Additionally, bison were quickly replaced by cattle, which should have provided an equivalent habitat.

A similar earlier hypothesis, proposed by Irving Cantrall and Frank Young in 1954, suggested that the extermination of the bison may have allowed locust populations to flourish by removing a competitive grazer, though historical records later disproved this<Citation source="lockwood" quote-index="4" />.

### The Dust Bowl

Though the Rocky Mountain Locust was eradicated (much to settlers' relief), grasshopper species capable of gregarious swarming remained in the United States. The High Plains grasshopper (_Dissosteira longipennis_)<Citation source="hopperwiki_highplains" /> and the Differential grasshopper (_Melanoplus differentialis_)<Citation source="differential_grasshopper" /> remained. During the Dust Bowl era, a combination of ecological decimation of local bird, toad, and snake populations and the [lack of wet springs](which usually kill locust eggs) allowed a new wave of grasshoppers to emerge. Consequently, the High Plains grasshopper and Migratory grasshopper (_Melanoplus sanguinipes_) surged across the same regions the Rocky Mountain Locust had previously ravaged, but this time the response was quick and militaristic<Citation source="migratory_grasshopper" /><Citation source="hopperwiki_highplains" />. These species were incapable of full phase polyphenism, and thus didn't exhibit the same level of swarming behavior as the Rocky Mountain Locust<Citation source="pfluger_braunig" />.

<SidebarTimeline>

Beginning in <Time alt="1933–1940 High Plains grasshopper plague">1933</Time>, local infestations of the High Plains grasshopper spread from Colorado and New Mexico and expanded each year until nearly 24 million acres across five states were affected<Citation source="hopperwiki_highplains" quote-index="0" />, with nymphs marching over entire counties, and adults swarming in the wind.

In <Time alt="Colorado National Guard mobilizes WPA and CCC against grasshoppers">1937</Time>, the Colorado National Guard along with members of the Works Progress Administration and the Civilian Conservation Corps mobilized to create over 31 million pounds of [toxic bait](Made from sawdust, bran, and sodium arsenite (essentially arsenic powder))<Citation source="national_guard" />. The National Guard then spread this poison across infested areas. They also fired flamethrowers from slow-moving trains, detonated explosives in fields, and dug trenches [to divert the pests](In an attempt that the insects would drown themselves in the trenches, or simply go a different direction - this didn't work very well). Though the poison was effective, men spreading the bait became sick (from the arsenic). Entomologists painted the insects to track their migration patterns. Overall these efforts lead to <Money from="9000000" year="1937">$9 million</Money> in crops<Citation source="national_guard" /> being saved.

By <Time alt="1940—Poison bait ends the outbreak, no swarms since!">1940</Time>, baiting combined with birds, weather, predators, and parasites finally brought the outbreak under control<Citation source="hopperwiki_highplains" quote-index="3" />. Today the High Plains grasshopper is considered very rare, and there has not been another swarming outbreak since.

</SidebarTimeline>

### Modern control of locusts

Modern locust control relies the idea of Phase Theory. The biology underlying [polyphenism](The biological switch from solitary to gregarious) was not fully understood until decades after the Rocky Mountain locust had gone extinct. In 1921, Russian entomologist <Hover content="Boris Petrovitch Uvarov (1886–1970) conducted field work in the Caucasus, then emigrated to England and directed the Anti-Locust Research Centre in London from 1945-1964, and was knighted in 1961!">Boris Uvarov</Hover> discovered that what had been thought to be [two separate species of grasshopper](_Locusta migratoria_ and _Locusta danica_) were actually a singular species in two phases, the solitarious and gregarious<Citation source="pfluger_braunig" />. This realization led to new control measures focusing on eradicating breeding grounds, and controlling population density, as the grasshoppers are harmless until they begin to swarm. Uvarov recognized this pattern in river valleys and in the Rocky Mountain locust. Modern locust control also relies on this framework. The [FAO Desert Locust Information Service](https://www.fao.org/locust-watch/activities/dlis-home/dlis/en) conducts satellite surveillance and ground surveys to send out early-warning alerts across Africa, the Arabian Peninsula and South Asia.<Citation source="fao_locust" /> Similar commissions around the world work to find solitarious populations and treat them with targeted insecticide before they enter their swarming alter egos.

---

The end of the Rocky Mountain Locust is unexpectedly quiet, an extinction of one of the most poopulous animals in history that nobody recognized for decades. The disastarous plagues of locusts that had showed up suddenly, vanished just as quickly, and all that remains of trillions are a few hundred frozen bodies in a glacier in Wyoming, and a few docile grasshoppers waiting for the right moment to rise again. In the end, the locusts wanted the same thing as the settlers, so naturally both fought for it: To expand, to colonize, and most of all, to have something to eat.
