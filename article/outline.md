## Transformation

- **Polyphenism**: [[Phenotype|phenotypic plasticity]]
  - Single genotype produces distinct phenotypes in response to environmental factors
  - Triggers:
    - Drought followed by abundant rain, vegetation allows breeding
    - Food shortage causes clustering:
      - Mechanosensory tactile stimulation on hind legs from being bumped
      - Smelling pheromones of other locusts
      - Seeing other locusts moving
    - Result
      - Triggers a pathway in the thoracic ganglia (CNS controlling locomotion) leads to increase in serotonin levels that causes gregarious behavior
        - Single locust injected with serotonin will start behaving gregariously within hours: https://journals.physiology.org/doi/full/10.1152/jn.00442.2025
      - Cephalic pathway - inside the insect's head
        - Each molt changes it
      - **Reversible**: If left in solitary for several hours it goes back: https://bioengineering.hyperbook.mcgill.ca/unlocking-locust-survival-a-chemical-analysis-of-locusts/#toc_1
      - Locust status passed down via epigenetic marks on eggs via [[DNA Methylation]]
        - In solitary mothers the Dop1 [[Dopamine]] receptor is suppressed, then in a gregarious mother methylation is reduced (hypomethylation)
- Neurotransmitters in the gregarious phase:
  - [[Serotonin]] released causes gregarious behavior, blocking it prevents the change and stimulating it causes the change.
  - Elevated dopamine, octopamine and tyramine regulate behaviors of attraction and repulsion to other locusts
- **Incompete Metamoprhosis**
  1.  Egg laid in soil in protective pods
  2.  Nymph hatch from eggs, wingless versions of adults. Grow through series of molts called instars developing wing buds that get larger
  3.  Functional wings and reproductive organs finally
  4.  **How long does this take? Diapause**

## Rocky Mountain Locust

Locusts - When we hear this word we think of swarming grasshopper creatures, taking to the skies, destroying crops, and spreading disease, but for those of us in most parts of the world, they are only that - a thought. Once there were approximately 12.5 trillion locusts in the United States - a mass the size of California of seething insects [engulfing the Midwest USA](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/73/Plate_II_The_locust_plague_in_the_United_States_%281877%29.jpg), but where did they go?

> "They began, toward night, dropping to the earth and it seemed as if we were in a big snowstorm where the air was filled with enormous-sized flakes", recounts Mary Lyon, a pioneer in Kansas recalling the Swam of 1874. "They devoured every green thing but the prairie grass".

The species - the [Rocky Mountain Locust](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a8/Melanoplus_spretusAnnReportAgExpStaUM1902B.jpg) (_Melanoplus spretus_) is now gone, the last specimen collected in 1904 only 40 years after the estimate of 12.5 trillion. They were so numerous that very few specimens were ever collected, nor were they extensively studied, driving modern [insect-historians](https://m.media-amazon.com/images/I/81QOkf8RSIL._AC_UF1000,1000_QL80_.jpg) to collect samples from glaciers that had engulfed them centuries ago[https://www.hcn.org/issues/issue-243/the-death-of-the-super-hopper/].

These huge swarms of locusts could consume over 4000 pounds of vegetation per hour, marching and swarming across vast swaths of land.

  - Plowing and destruction of breeding grounds

    - Proposed by Paul Riegert - Canadian entomologist - "Riegert advocated precisely the opposite consequence. He suggested that the changes in the landscape following the extirpation of bison were detrimental, over the long term, for the locust" - Lockwood

The egg beds lay between the overly wet, silty streamsides and the sere, rocky hillsides. During their plundering of the prairie farms, the locusts laid their eggs in soils that mimicked the fertile river valleys of the Rockies" - Lockwood
        - Bison were replaced by cattle - The influx of cattle would have sustained the locusts

        - Historical records disproved this as well - "Our best guess is that there were 30 to 60 million bison in North America, prior to European settlement.
